Master’s method

“Cookbook” for solving recurrences of the form:

T(n)=aT(nb)+f(n)T(n)=aT(\frac{n}{b})+f(n)\\ T(n)=aT(bn​)+f(n)

where, a≥1,b>1a\geq 1 , b>1a≥1,b>1 , and f(n)>0f(n)>0f(n)>0

Idea: compare f(n)f(n)f(n) with nlog⁡ban^{\log_ba}nlogb​a .

Why nlog⁡ban^{\log_b^a}nlogba​ ?

T(n)=aT(nb)=a2T(nb2)⋮=aiT(nbi)∀iT(n)=aT(\frac{n}{b})\\ =a^2T(\frac{n}{b^2})\\ \vdots \\ =a^iT(\frac{n}{b^i}) \hspace{1em} \forall i T(n)=aT(bn​)=a2T(b2n​)⋮=aiT(bin​)∀i

Assume n=bk⇒k=log⁡bnn=b^k \Rightarrow k=\log_bnn=bk⇒k=logb​n

At the end of iteration i=ki=ki=k :
T(n)=alog⁡bnT(bibi)=alog⁡bnT(1)=Θ(alog⁡bn)=Θ(nlog⁡ba)T(n)=a^{\log_bn}T(\frac{b^i}{b^i})=a^{\log_bn}T(1)=\Theta(a^{\log_bn})=\Theta(n^{\log_ba})T(n)=alogb​nT(bibi​)=alogb​nT(1)=Θ(alogb​n)=Θ(nlogb​a) .

Simpler case

Let T(n)T(n)T(n) be a monotonically increasing function that satisfies:

T(n)=aT(n/b)+f(n)T(1)=cT(n)=aT(n/b)+f(n)\\ T(1)=c T(n)=aT(n/b)+f(n)T(1)=c

where a≥1,b≥2,c>0a\geq 1,b\geq 2,c>0a≥1,b≥2,c>0 . If f(n)f(n)f(n) is Θ(nd)\Theta(n^d)Θ(nd) where d≥0d\geq 0d≥0 then

Pitfalls

You cannot use the Master Theorem if :

  1. T(n)T(n)T(n) is not monotone, e.g. T(n)=sin(x)T(n)=sin(x)T(n)=sin(x)

  2. f(n)f(n)f(n) is not a polynomial, e.g. T(n)=2T(n/2)+2nT(n)=2T(n/2)+2^nT(n)=2T(n/2)+2n

  3. bbb cannot be expressed as a constant, e.g. T(n)=T(n)T(n)=T(\sqrt{n})T(n)=T(n​)

Note that the Master Theorem does not solve the recurrence equation.
